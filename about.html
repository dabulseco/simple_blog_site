Dylan Bulseco, Ph.D.

Senior Vice President & Principal Scientist
Institute for Future Intelligence

Studying how artificial intelligence reshapes pedagogy, scientific practice, and human expertise in education and the life sciences.

Professional Background

Dylan Bulseco is a Senior Vice President and Principal Scientist at the Institute for Future Intelligence (IFI). He holds a Ph.D. in Biochemistry and Biophysics from Oregon State University and brings over two decades of experience in fluorescence-based instrumentation, reagents, and analytical applications spanning research, diagnostics, medical devices, and therapeutics.

His career bridges academic research and commercialization, with leadership roles as Principal Investigator, co-Principal Investigator, or senior personnel on federally funded projects supported by the National Institutes of Health (NIH), the National Science Foundation (NSF), and the Department of Homeland Security (DHS).

Focus on AI in Education

At IFI, Dylan’s work centers on studying the impact of artificial intelligence on education, particularly in STEM and life sciences contexts. His research examines how AI tools—such as intelligent tutors, generative models, and data-driven learning systems—transform pedagogy, assessment, and knowledge construction.

Rather than treating AI as a replacement for educators or expertise, his work emphasizes AI as a pedagogical partner: a tool that augments human reasoning, supports inquiry-based learning, and enables educators to design more adaptive, reflective, and equitable learning environments.

This research draws on transcript analysis, learning sciences theory, and human–AI interaction studies to understand not only what students learn, but how learning unfolds through dialogue, reflection, and guided exploration in AI-enhanced educational ecosystems.

Cultural Relevance and Workforce Development

As a Native Hawaiian, Dylan is deeply committed to culturally responsive science education and workforce development. His projects integrate computational science, data science, and artificial intelligence into life sciences education in ways that respect local knowledge systems and community contexts.

He has been actively involved in initiatives supporting science education and workforce pathways in both Massachusetts and Hawaiʻi, with a particular focus on community colleges, place-based learning, and experiential research programs that prepare students for emerging STEM careers.
